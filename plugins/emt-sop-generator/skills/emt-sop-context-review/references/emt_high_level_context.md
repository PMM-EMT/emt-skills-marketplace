# EMT Domain Context
_Source: WHO Classification and Minimum Standards for Emergency Medical Teams, 2021_

## Identity
EMTs are temporary, deployable surge medical capabilities that **supplement** (not replace) national health systems during disasters, outbreaks, conflicts, and public health emergencies. EMTs must be self-sufficient, trained, and coordinated. Staff profiles: doctors, nurses, paramedics, midwives, pharmacists, logisticians, WASH specialists, coordinators. Sponsors: national, international, governmental, NGO, military, Red Cross/Crescent, UN-contracted, or private.

## EMT Types

| Type | Model | Core Services |
|------|-------|---------------|
| Type 1 Mobile | Mobile, multi-location | Daylight outpatient: triage, stabilization, referral, primary care |
| Type 1 Fixed | Fixed facility | Same as Type 1 Mobile from a single site |
| Type 2 | Inpatient surgical | Type 1 + inpatient acute care, general and obstetric surgery |
| Type 3 | Inpatient referral | Type 2 + complex referral-level inpatient and ICU care |
| Specialized Cell | Embedded in facilities or other EMTs | Focused support: outbreak, surgical, rehab, MHPSS, reproductive/newborn, or operational |

## Guiding Principles
- **Safe:** avoid harm from care intended to help
- **Equitable:** access for all, especially vulnerable groups
- **Ethical:** evidence-based, medically ethical care
- **Accountable:** to patients, communities, host authorities, donors, and own organization
- **Coordinated:** operate through national emergency management mechanisms
- **Appropriate:** tailor to needs, context, and community values
- Humanitarian alignment: humanity, neutrality, impartiality, operational independence

## Operating Logic
- MoH or designated health authority holds primary authority at all times
- Deploy only through: request → acceptance → registration → tasking → coordination
- National EMTs are the fastest first-surge mechanism; international EMTs support when national capacity is overwhelmed
- Integrate with local referral pathways, surveillance systems, and public health response
- Do not create parallel systems unless explicitly requested and coordinated
- Plan for handover, demobilization, and care continuity from day one

## Deployment Lifecycle
1. Offer → Acceptance → Arrival & registration
2. Medical licensing/authorization to practise
3. Tasking to site of operation
4. Service establishment → Monitoring & reporting → Re-tasking if required
5. Handover → Exit & demobilization → After-action review & readiness restoration

## Clinical Standards
All EMTs must provide: triage, assessment, stabilization, referral, IPC, patient records, pharmacy management, and public health awareness.

- Scope extends beyond trauma: communicable/noncommunicable diseases, reproductive/newborn care, child health, rehabilitation, mental health, public health
- Use national clinical protocols; if unavailable, use internationally accepted guidance adapted to context
- SOPs must cover: patient safety, consent, confidentiality, pain management, referral criteria, handover, and follow-up
- Match services strictly to declared capability and assigned tasking
- Do not perform procedures outside declared scope, staff competence, licensing, or available equipment

## Coordination
- MoH leads or authorizes coordination, ideally through health Emergency Operations Centre (EOC)
- EMTCC functions: registration, tasking, reporting, referral coordination, field monitoring, information management
- EMTs must share: operational status, capability, location, patient numbers, referral needs, gaps, risks, and exit plans
- Military, NGO, Red Cross/Crescent, and specialist teams should coordinate through the EMT mechanism when providing direct care

## Records & Reporting
- Maintain confidential patient records; provide patients discharge/referral documents
- Report to health authorities using national forms; if unavailable, use the agreed EMT Minimum Data Set (MDS)
- Reporting supports: surveillance, referrals, public health action, coordination, and situational awareness
- Research requires: patient consent + national authority approval + ethical approval

## Operational Requirements

### Self-Sufficiency
- Cover independently: food, water, shelter, fuel, power, communications, medical supplies, pharmaceuticals, PPE, transport, and waste management
- Local procurement only when it does not harm local availability, prices, or response capacity
- SOPs must state: assumptions, minimum stock levels, resupply triggers, and contingency options

### Logistics & WASH
- **Logistics:** power, fuel, communications, transport, fleet, food, warehouse, pharmacy supply chain, safety, security, structures, site planning, and demobilization
- **WASH:** water supply, hygiene, environmental cleaning, healthcare waste, sanitation, vector/pest control, dead body management
- Redundancy required for: communications, power, water, oxygen, cold chain, and patient referral

### Safety & Security
- SOPs must include: risk assessment, security plan, incident reporting, critical incident management, evacuation/extraction, hibernation, fire safety, occupational safety, and staff welfare
- Duty of care applies to all staff and volunteers
- Facilities must protect: patients, staff, records, pharmaceuticals, controlled drugs, equipment, and waste areas

## Community & Protection
- Use local languages and culturally appropriate communication
- Address in facility design and SOPs: gender, age, disability, child protection, SGBV, privacy, dignity, and safe access
- Ensure meaningful access for persons with disabilities (PWD) and reduced mobility

## Public Health Role
- Contribute to disease surveillance and early warning; identify and report communicable disease risks
- Apply IPC across all services; support health promotion and community engagement
- Report observed wider needs (WASH, nutrition, vector control, protection, referral gaps) without assuming responsibility unless specifically tasked

## Operating Environment Constraints
EMTs commonly face: damaged infrastructure, disrupted supply chains, overwhelmed local staff, limited diagnostics, disrupted power/water/communications/cold chain, mass casualty events, population displacement, insecurity, and fluctuating caseloads. SOPs must be updated as situational awareness improves.

## Key Terminology

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Capability | What the team is able to provide |
| Capacity | Volume or scale the team can provide |
| Tasking | Assignment to a role, service, or location |
| Handover | Formal transfer of patients, services, records, or responsibilities |
| Demobilization | Planned withdrawal and restoration of team readiness |
| Referral pathway | Agreed route to higher, lower, or specialized care |
| Self-sufficient | Able to operate without burdening local systems |
| Minimum standards | Required baseline performance |
| Recommendations | Optimal or context-dependent improvements |
